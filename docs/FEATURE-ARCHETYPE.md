# Feature Spec: Neighborhood Archetype + Social Card

---

## 1. The Problem Worth Solving

Locale reports are comprehensive, editorial-quality, and genuinely useful. They are also *unshareable in practice*. The share buttons exist, the OG image works, the URL is clean — but there's no atomic unit of identity that makes someone *want* to share. A 2,000-word report with five data sections doesn't fit in a tweet, a text message, or an Instagram story. The report is the product; what's missing is the *artifact*.

Consider how people actually share location-related content today:

- "I'm in a walkable neighborhood" — vague, no hook
- "My rent is $2,400 for a 1-bedroom" — one data point, no identity
- "I live in Brooklyn" — identity but no insight

Now consider: **"My neighborhood is *The Brownstone Belt* — where stoops are social infrastructure and the bodega knows your order."**

That's shareable. That's identity. That's the feature.

---

## 2. The Insight: Identity as Distribution

The most shared content formats on the internet have one thing in common: they give people a **label that feels true**. Spotify Wrapped, Myers-Briggs, "Which character are you?", Enneagram — these all work because:

1. **The label is personal.** It's about *your* data, *your* place, *your* identity.
2. **The label is concise.** It fits in a screenshot, a bio, a caption.
3. **The label invites comparison.** "What did you get?" is the most powerful question in viral design.
4. **The label is slightly surprising.** It tells you something you already felt but hadn't articulated — so you share it to say "this is so accurate."

The neighborhood archetype system applies this pattern to Locale. Every report gets classified into a distinct personality type — not a score, not a rating, but a *character* — and that character becomes the unit of sharing.

**This is the strategic context for the case study:** the archetype isn't a gimmick bolted onto the report. It's the feature that transforms Locale from "a tool I used" into "a thing I shared." The report is for the user. The archetype is for the user's audience.

## 3. Feature Design

### 3.1 Neighborhood Archetype

Every completed report receives an AI-classified **archetype** — a 2–5 word personality label that captures what makes this place feel like itself.

**Components:**

| Element | Example | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Archetype label** | *The Brownstone Belt* | The shareable identity. Title case. 2–5 words. |
| **Tagline** | *Where stoops are social infrastructure and the bodega knows your order.* | One sentence that makes the label feel earned. Vivid, specific, slightly poetic. |
| **Defining traits** | `$2,800 rent · 83% walk to work · 47 restaurants nearby` | Three data-backed phrases that ground the archetype in reality. These are derived from the report data, not generated by the AI. |

**Why not a fixed taxonomy?** We considered defining 12 archetypes (like zodiac signs) and slotting every neighborhood into one. We rejected this because:

- Fixed bins force false equivalence. "College town" Ann Arbor and "college town" College Station are meaningfully different. A generative label captures *The Campus Orbit* vs. *Friday Night Lights Country* — the model can encode nuance that bins can't.
- A fixed taxonomy caps virality. With 12 options, most people share the same result. With generative labels, your neighborhood gets a label that feels *yours*. (Spotify Wrapped works partly because "your top artist" is unique to you, not one of 12 options.)
- It demonstrates a more interesting AI capability. Classification into generative categories is a harder, more compelling use of the model than slotting into predefined bins. This matters for the case study.

**But we still need consistency.** Fully unconstrained generation would produce inconsistent quality — some labels poetic, some generic, some nonsensical. The solution: provide ~16 **seed archetypes** as examples in the prompt, and instruct the model to use one if it fits, or generate a new one in the same style. This gives structure without rigidity.

### 3.2 The Vibe Spectrum

Each archetype is accompanied by a **Vibe Spectrum** — five normalized scores (0–100) on axes that capture the character of a place:

| Axis | Low end (0) | High end (100) | Primary data signals |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Walkable** | Car-centric | Everything on foot | POI count + category diversity, isochrone area, walk/transit commute % |
| **Buzzing** | Quiet residential | Never sleeps | Dining + nightlife POI count, population density |
| **Settled** | Rapid change | Deep roots | Pre-1950 housing %, owner-occupied %, median age |
| **Accessible** | Premium pricing | Affordable | Median rent vs. national, median home value vs. national, income-to-rent ratio |
| **Diverse** | Homogeneous | Mosaic | Racial/ethnic distribution evenness (Simpson's diversity index), POI category variety |

**Why five axes?** A pentagon is the sweet spot: enough dimensions to feel meaningful, few enough to be instantly readable. Three feels like a rating. Seven becomes noise. Five is scannable, drawable, and comparable.

**Why these axes?** They map to the questions people actually ask when evaluating a neighborhood: Can I walk places? Is it lively? Is it established? Can I afford it? Who lives here? Every other dimension (transit quality, school ratings, safety) is either a sub-signal of these five or requires data we don't have.

**The vibe spectrum is the hero visual on the social card.** A filled pentagon/radar chart is immediately recognizable, aesthetically distinct, and invites comparison. Two pentagons side by side tell you more in a glance than a paragraph of stats.

## 5. AI Classification Prompt Design

The archetype classification is a **separate AI call** from the narrative generation — not embedded in the narrative prompt. Reasons:

1. **Different output structure.** The narrative is free-form prose. The archetype requires structured JSON output (label, tagline, spectrum scores, traits). Mixing these in one prompt degrades both.
2. **Different temperature.** The narrative benefits from temperature 0.7 (creative, varied). The classification should use temperature 0.3 (consistent, stable). A neighborhood should get roughly the same archetype across multiple runs.
3. **Independent caching.** The archetype can be generated and stored alongside the narrative. If narrative generation fails, the archetype might still succeed (and vice versa).
4. **Eval-friendly.** A separate call with structured output is much easier to evaluate for consistency and quality than extracting classifications from prose.

### Prompt structure

**System prompt:**

```
You are a neighborhood classification system. Given structured data about a
US neighborhood (demographics, housing, economics, walkability, and nearby
amenities), you assign it a personality archetype — a vivid, memorable label
that captures what makes this place feel like itself.

Your classification must be grounded in the data provided. Do not hallucinate
or assume facts not in evidence. The archetype should feel specific to this
place, not generic.

SEED ARCHETYPES (use one if it fits, or create a new label in the same style):
[...seed catalog from Section 4...]

OUTPUT FORMAT (strict JSON):
{
  "archetype": "The Brownstone Belt",
  "tagline": "Where stoops are social infrastructure and the bodega knows your order.",
  "vibeSpectrum": {
    "walkable": 85,
    "buzzing": 62,
    "settled": 78,
    "accessible": 35,
    "diverse": 71
  },
  "definingTraits": [
    "$2,800 median rent",
    "83% walk or take transit",
    "47 restaurants within 10 min"
  ],
  "reasoning": "Brief 1-2 sentence explanation of why this archetype fits."
}

RULES:
- archetype: 2-5 words, title case, evocative but not cutesy
- tagline: one sentence, vivid and specific to this place, no cliches
- vibeSpectrum: each value 0-100, must be justified by the data
- definingTraits: exactly 3 short phrases, each grounded in a specific data point
- reasoning: for internal eval only, not displayed to users
- Respond with ONLY the JSON object, no markdown fencing or commentary
```

**User prompt:** Same data payload as the narrative prompt (reuse `buildUserPrompt(data)` from `narrative.ts`).

**Model:** `claude-sonnet-4-6` (same as narrative)
**Temperature:** 0.3
**Max tokens:** 500

### Output type

```typescript
interface ArchetypeResult {
  archetype: string;       // "The Brownstone Belt"
  tagline: string;         // "Where stoops are social..."
  vibeSpectrum: {
    walkable: number;      // 0-100
    buzzing: number;       // 0-100
    settled: number;       // 0-100
    accessible: number;    // 0-100
    diverse: number;       // 0-100
  };
  definingTraits: [string, string, string];
  reasoning: string;       // internal only
}
```

### Persistence

The archetype result is stored in the existing `reports.data` JSONB column as a new `archetype` field on the `ReportData` interface:

```typescript
export interface ReportData {
  // ...existing fields...
  archetype: ArchetypeResult | null;
}
```

The archetype is generated **after** the data is fetched but **before** the narrative, so the narrative prompt can reference the archetype label if desired. The generation flow becomes:

```
data fetch (parallel) → archetype classification → narrative generation
```

If archetype classification fails, it falls back to `null` and the report renders without it (graceful degradation, consistent with Census/isochrone/POI failure handling).

## 6. Social Card Generation (Technical)

### Approach: Satori + @vercel/og

Use [Satori](https://github.com/vercel/satori) (Vercel's JSX-to-SVG renderer) via the `@vercel/og` package to generate card images server-side. This is the same library that powers Vercel's OG image generation and is well-suited for Next.js.

### Route: `GET /api/report/[slug]/card`

Query parameters:
- `format=og` (default) → 1200x630 OG card
- `format=story` → 1080x1920 story card

Response: `Content-Type: image/png` with `Cache-Control: public, s-maxage=31536000, immutable` (cards are deterministic for a given report).

### Implementation sketch

```typescript
// src/app/api/report/[slug]/card/route.tsx
import { ImageResponse } from "@vercel/og";

export async function GET(
  request: Request,
  { params }: { params: { slug: string } }
) {
  const { searchParams } = new URL(request.url);
  const format = searchParams.get("format") === "story" ? "story" : "og";

  // 1. Fetch report from DB (same pattern as report page)
  // 2. Extract archetype data from report.data.archetype
  // 3. Render JSX → ImageResponse

  const dimensions = format === "story"
    ? { width: 1080, height: 1920 }
    : { width: 1200, height: 630 };

  return new ImageResponse(
    <CardLayout
      format={format}
      archetype={archetype}
      address={address}
      cityState={cityState}
    />,
    {
      ...dimensions,
      fonts: [/* Playfair Display + Inter loaded from static files */],
    },
  );
}
```

### Pentagon chart rendering

The vibe spectrum pentagon is drawn as an SVG `<polygon>` with computed vertices. Each axis maps to one of five points equally spaced around a circle. The vertex distance from center = `score / 100 * radius`.

```
     Walkable (top)
        /\
       /  \
Diverse    Buzzing
      |    |
Accessible  Settled
```

Two polygons are rendered:
1. **Background polygon** — all vertices at full radius, stroke only, light gray. Shows the "maximum" shape.
2. **Data polygon** — vertices at actual scores, filled with accent green at 15% opacity, stroked at full opacity. Shows the neighborhood's shape.

This is pure SVG math — no charting library needed. Works in both Satori (for image generation) and React (for in-page rendering).

### Font loading

Satori requires font files to be loaded explicitly (it doesn't have access to Google Fonts at runtime). Bundle Playfair Display Bold and Inter Regular/Medium as static `.woff` files in `public/fonts/`. These are already used by the app — we just need the raw files for Satori.

## 7. Integration Points

### 7.1 Report generation flow (modified)

Current:
```
POST /api/report/generate
  → fetch Census + isochrone + POI in parallel
  → insert report (status: generating)
  → redirect to /report/[slug]
  → NarrativeTrigger POSTs to /api/report/[slug]/narrative
  → narrative streams, report set to complete
```

New:
```
POST /api/report/generate
  → fetch Census + isochrone + POI in parallel
  → insert report (status: generating)
  → redirect to /report/[slug]
  → ArchetypeTrigger POSTs to /api/report/[slug]/archetype  ← NEW
  → archetype classified, stored in report.data.archetype
  → NarrativeTrigger POSTs to /api/report/[slug]/narrative
  → narrative streams, report set to complete
```

The archetype classification runs first because it's fast (~1-2s at temperature 0.3 with 500 max tokens) and the result can optionally inform the narrative prompt.

### 7.2 New API route: `POST /api/report/[slug]/archetype`

- Validates report exists and is in `generating` status
- Calls Claude with the classification prompt
- Parses and validates the JSON response
- Updates `report.data.archetype` in the JSONB column
- Returns 200 with the archetype result (or 500 on failure)

### 7.3 New client component: `ArchetypeTrigger`

Same pattern as `NarrativeTrigger` — a client component that fires the archetype POST on mount, then the narrative POST on archetype completion (or timeout). Renders nothing visible.

### 7.4 New client component: `ArchetypeBanner`

Renders the archetype label, tagline, and vibe spectrum pentagon on the report page. Accepts `ArchetypeResult` as a prop. Wrapped in `SectionErrorBoundary`. Animates in with `fadeUp` variant.

### 7.5 OG image update

The report page's `generateMetadata` function currently returns a Mapbox Static Images URL as the OG image. Update this to point to the new card route:

```typescript
openGraph: {
  images: [`/api/report/${slug}/card?format=og`],
}
```

This means every shared link automatically gets the archetype card as its preview image. **This is the highest-leverage change in the entire feature** — it turns every existing share into a richer, more clickable artifact.

### 7.6 Story card download

Add a "Download Card" button to `ShareControls`. On click, fetch `/api/report/[slug]/card?format=story` as a blob, create an object URL, and trigger a download. File name: `locale-{slug}.png`.

### 7.7 Homepage featured cards

The existing featured cards on the homepage show a Mapbox thumbnail. Once these reports have archetypes, the cards could show the archetype label as a badge overlay — making the homepage itself more compelling and demonstrating the feature before the user generates their own report.

## 8. Evaluation Strategy

Archetype classification quality is critical — a bad archetype destroys trust faster than no archetype. The eval strategy operates at three levels:

### 8.1 Golden dataset validation

Run the archetype classification against all 20 golden addresses. For each, evaluate:

- **Label appropriateness:** Does the archetype label match the `description` field in `golden-addresses.ts`? (Human review)
- **Tagline specificity:** Could this tagline apply to a different neighborhood? If yes, it's too generic. (Human review)
- **Spectrum accuracy:** Do the five scores match intuition? Is Manhattan's "walkable" score near 100? Is Galena's "buzzing" score near 0? (Human review with sanity bounds)
- **Trait grounding:** Are all three defining traits verifiable from the report data? (Automated check)

### 8.2 Consistency test

Run the same address through classification 5 times. The archetype label should be identical or semantically equivalent (e.g., "The Urban Engine" and "The Metro Engine" are acceptable variation; "The Urban Engine" and "Cul-de-Sac Country" are not). The vibe spectrum scores should be within ±10 points across runs.

**This is the most important eval.** If the same address gets wildly different archetypes on different runs, the feature isn't trustworthy. Temperature 0.3 + strong prompt constraints should keep variance low, but this must be verified.

### 8.3 Edge case handling

Test with reports where data sources partially failed:
- Census only (no POI, no isochrone) → archetype should still work, spectrum axes that depend on missing data should default to 50 (neutral)
- POI only → limited but functional
- All sources failed → archetype should return null (don't classify what you can't see)

### 8.4 Eval script extension

Extend the existing `npm run test:eval` script to include archetype classification. For each golden address, generate the archetype and output it alongside the narrative for human review.

## 11. Open Questions

1. **Should the narrative prompt reference the archetype?** If the archetype is generated first, we could include it in the narrative's context: "This neighborhood has been classified as *The Brownstone Belt*." This might help the narrative feel more cohesive — or it might make the narrative feel derivative. Test both approaches against the golden dataset.

2. **Archetype for existing reports.** ~N reports already exist in the database without archetypes. Options: (a) backfill via a migration script that re-classifies each one, (b) classify lazily on next page view, (c) leave them without archetypes. Recommendation: (b) lazy classification, triggered by a component that checks for `data.archetype === null` on a completed report.

3. **Should the vibe spectrum be AI-generated or computed?** The current design has the AI generate the spectrum scores. Alternative: compute them deterministically from the data (e.g., walkable = f(POI count, transit commute %, isochrone area)) and only have the AI generate the label + tagline. Tradeoffs: AI-generated scores are more nuanced but less consistent; computed scores are deterministic but may feel crude. Recommendation: start with AI-generated, add a validation step that bounds scores against computed sanity checks.

4. **Card caching strategy.** The card image is deterministic for a given report, so it can be cached aggressively (`immutable`). But if we ever allow report regeneration or archetype re-classification, we'd need cache-busting. Options: (a) include a version hash in the URL, (b) use `stale-while-revalidate`, (c) accept that regenerated reports keep the old card until CDN TTL expires.

5. **Pentagon chart accessibility.** The pentagon is purely visual. For screen readers, include an `aria-label` that lists all five scores as text: "Walkable: 85, Buzzing: 62, Settled: 78, Accessible: 35, Diverse: 71." In the Satori-generated image, this information is conveyed by the defining traits text.
